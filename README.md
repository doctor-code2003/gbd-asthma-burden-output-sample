# Study Overview

The analysis evaluated asthma burden using GBD 2021 estimates across global, regional, and national levels. The main outcomes were incidence, prevalence, mortality, and disability-adjusted life years (DALYs). Temporal trends were summarized using average annual percent change (AAPC), and burden patterns were examined by geography, age, sex, and socio-demographic index (SDI). Projection outputs were generated using a Bayesian age-period-cohort (BAPC) framework.

---

## Abbreviations

| Abbreviation | Meaning |
|---|---|
| ASIR | Age-standardized incidence rate |
| ASPR | Age-standardized prevalence rate |
| ASMR | Age-standardized mortality rate |
| ASDR | Age-standardized DALY rate |
| DALYs | Disability-adjusted life years |
| AAPC | Average annual percent change |
| SDI | Socio-demographic index |
| BAPC | Bayesian age-period-cohort |

# Results

## 1. Global Burden Trend, 1990–2021

Globally, asthma burden declined across the four major age-standardized indicators between 1990 and 2021. The AAPC values were negative for incidence, prevalence, mortality, and DALYs, indicating an overall reduction in standardized asthma burden over time.

| Indicator | Global AAPC |
|---|---:|
| ASIR | -1.147 |
| ASPR | -1.619 |
| ASMR | -1.932 |
| ASDR | -1.872 |

The global asthma prevalence rate decreased from **5,568 per 100,000 population in 1990** to **3,340 per 100,000 population in 2021**. The DALY rate also decreased from **476 per 100,000 population** to **265 per 100,000 population** during the same period.

**Interpretation:**  
The global standardized burden of asthma improved over the study period. However, the persistence of substantial prevalence and DALY rates in 2021 indicates that asthma remains an important chronic respiratory disease burden despite long-term improvements.

## 2. Regional Ranking of Asthma Burden in 2021

The top-ranked regions differed by indicator. High-income North America had the highest incidence and prevalence-related burden, whereas Oceania had the highest mortality and DALY-related burden.

### Table 1. Top Three Regions by Age-Standardized Rates in 2021

| Indicator | Location | ASR (95% uncertainty interval) | AAPC (95% confidence interval) |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| Incidence | High-income North America | 1403.64 (1137.64–1766.66) | -0.017 (-0.221 to 0.187) |
| Incidence | Caribbean | 1193.84 (1000.88–1445.97) | -0.282 (-0.309 to -0.256)* |
| Incidence | Central Europe | 898.73 (725.29–1136.93) | 0.034 (-0.076 to 0.144) |
| Prevalence | High-income North America | 9717.74 (8485.10–11226.93) | 0.088 (-0.014 to 0.191) |
| Prevalence | Australasia | 7747.21 (6479.30–9107.51) | -1.652 (-1.784 to -1.519)* |
| Prevalence | Caribbean | 7638.48 (6722.22–8563.44) | -0.624 (-0.688 to -0.559)* |
| Deaths | Oceania | 33.98 (24.05–51.08) | -1.027 (-1.075 to -0.979)* |
| Deaths | South Asia | 17.68 (12.55–26.32) | -1.352 (-1.880 to -0.822)* |
| Deaths | Central Sub-Saharan Africa | 15.79 (8.99–37.27) | -1.210 (-1.306 to -1.114)* |
| DALYs | Oceania | 847.59 (626.75–1212.54) | -1.157 (-1.224 to -1.089)* |

| DALYs | Caribbean | 468.60 (349.60–628.75) | -1.038 (-1.145 to -0.930)* |

\* Statistically significant AAPC.

**Interpretation:**  
High-income North America ranked first for both ASIR and ASPR, indicating a high measured occurrence burden. In contrast, Oceania had the highest ASMR and ASDR, showing that the greatest mortality and disability burden was concentrated in a different region.

This separation between occurrence indicators and outcome indicators is a key epidemiological finding. High incidence or prevalence may reflect greater detection, diagnosis, or chronic disease survival, whereas high ASMR and ASDR suggest more severe outcomes, lower disease control, poorer access to care, or higher vulnerability to fatal and disabling asthma outcomes.

## 3. Countries and Regions with Increasing Burden Trends

Although global age-standardized asthma burden declined overall, several countries and regions showed increasing AAPC values for specific indicators.

### Table 2. Countries and Regions with Increasing AAPC, 1990–2021

| Indicator | Location | AAPC (95% confidence interval) |
|---|---|---:|
| ASIR | Oman | 0.317 (0.245–0.388) |
| ASIR | Poland | 0.284 (0.187–0.381) |
| ASIR | Barbados | 0.256 (0.164–0.347) |
| ASIR | Saudi Arabia | 0.111 (0.043–0.180) |
| ASIR | Bermuda | 0.084 (0.054–0.114) |
| ASPR | Oman | 0.138 (0.061–0.216) |
| ASPR | United States of America | 0.155 (0.069–0.241) |
| ASMR | Zimbabwe | 0.396 (0.033–0.760) |
| ASDR | Zimbabwe | 0.567 (0.309–0.825) |
| ASDR | Lesotho | 0.351 (0.146–0.557) |

**Interpretation:**  
Oman showed the largest increase in ASIR, followed by Poland and Barbados. These findings indicate that asthma incidence did not decline uniformly across all locations, despite the overall global improvement.

The United States showed an increasing ASPR, indicating a rising prevalence burden. This may reflect increasing diagnosis, prolonged disease duration, population-level exposure changes, or a sustained high chronic disease burden.

The most concerning signals are the increases in ASMR and ASDR in Zimbabwe and the increase in ASDR in Lesotho. Increases in mortality and DALY rates represent worsening outcome burden rather than only increased disease detection. These locations may require closer public-health attention because rising ASMR or ASDR suggests possible gaps in asthma control, treatment access, acute care, or prevention.

## 6. Age- and Sex-Specific DALY Burden

### Figure 3. Number and rate of asthma DALYs by age and sex in 2021  
**File:** `asthma_dalys_age_sex_2021.png`

<img src="assets/asthma_dalys_age_sex_2021.png" width="850">

**Interpretation:**  
Figure 3 shows a bimodal DALY burden pattern. In absolute DALY counts, the male burden peaked in children aged **5–9 years** with approximately **924,212 DALYs**, while the female burden peaked in adults aged **55–59 years** with approximately **856,216 DALYs**.

The rate-based burden increased again in older age groups, indicating that elderly populations experience a high burden relative to population size. This suggests that childhood asthma contributes heavily to total DALYs, while older adults carry a high rate-based burden, likely due to comorbidity, vulnerability to complications, and greater risk of severe outcomes.

The sex pattern also changed by age. Males had a higher burden in younger age groups, whereas female burden became more prominent in adulthood. This supports the need for age- and sex-specific prevention and management strategies.

<img src="assets/asthma_aapc_by_sdi_bar_chart.png" width="850">

**Interpretation:**  
Figure 4 shows that AAPC values were generally negative across global and SDI-level groups, confirming the overall decline in age-standardized asthma burden. However, the magnitude of decline differed by SDI group and by burden indicator.

The decline was more pronounced for mortality-related indicators in higher-SDI settings, consistent with improvements in asthma management, access to medications, emergency care, and long-term disease control. In contrast, lower-SDI settings retained a more severe outcome profile, with higher mortality and DALY burden despite lower measured prevalence or incidence.

This pattern indicates that SDI modifies the relationship between asthma occurrence and asthma outcomes.

## 8. Temporal Trends by SDI Level and Sex

### Figure 5. Temporal trends of asthma burden indicators by SDI level and sex, 1990–2021  
**File:** `asthma_sdi_temporal_trends.png`

<img src="assets/asthma_sdi_temporal_trends.png" width="850">

**Interpretation:**  
Figure 5 shows that high-SDI regions consistently had higher ASIR and ASPR, whereas low-SDI regions had higher ASMR and ASDR. This indicates that higher-development settings experience greater measured occurrence burden, while lower-development settings experience greater fatal and disabling burden.

In 2021, low-SDI regions had an ASDR of **527.4 per 100,000** and an ASMR of **16.5 per 100,000**, representing the highest outcome burden across SDI groups. In contrast, high-SDI regions had lower mortality and DALY rates despite higher incidence and prevalence.

The most pronounced decline in ASMR occurred in high-SDI regions, decreasing from **4.8 per 100,000 in 1990** to **0.9 per 100,000 in 2021**. The largest decline in ASIR occurred in low-middle-SDI regions, decreasing from **730.7 per 100,000 in 1990** to **443.6 per 100,000 in 2021**.

Overall, this figure shows that asthma burden has two distinct dimensions: occurrence burden and outcome burden. These dimensions move differently across SDI groups.

## 9. Association Between SDI and Asthma Burden

### Figure 6. SDI correlation analysis for asthma burden indicators  
**File:** `asthma_sdi_correlation_scatter.png`

<img src="assets/asthma_sdi_correlation_scatter.png" width="850">

**Interpretation:**  
Figure 6 shows divergent relationships between SDI and asthma burden indicators. ASPR was positively associated with SDI, whereas ASDR and ASMR were strongly negatively associated with SDI. ASIR showed no clear linear association.

| Indicator | Correlation with SDI | P value | Result interpretation |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| ASPR | R = 0.282 | p = 1.41e-04 | Higher SDI was associated with higher measured prevalence |
| ASIR | R = 0.0141 | p = 0.8522 | No clear linear association with incidence |
| ASDR | R = -0.600 | p = 8.64e-19 | Higher SDI was associated with lower DALY burden |
| ASMR | R = -0.644 | p = 3.12e-22 | Higher SDI was associated with lower mortality burden |
| AAPC of ASPR | R = -0.059 | p = 0.4382 | No significant association |
| AAPC of ASIR | R = 0.176 | p = 0.01984 | Weak positive association |
| AAPC of ASDR | R = -0.116 | p = 0.1251 | No significant association |
| AAPC of ASMR | R = -0.600 | p = 1.61e-18 | Strong negative association |

The main result is the contrast between measured prevalence and outcome burden. Higher-SDI settings showed higher measured prevalence but lower mortality and DALY rates. This likely reflects better case detection, better long-term asthma control, greater medication availability, and stronger healthcare systems. Lower-SDI settings may have underdiagnosis but worse outcomes among diagnosed or symptomatic patients.

## 10. High BMI-Attributable Asthma DALYs

### Figure 7. Proportion of asthma DALYs attributable to high BMI by region, year, and sex  
**File:** `asthma_high_bmi_attribution.png`

<img src="assets/asthma_high_bmi_attribution.png" width="850">

**Interpretation:**  
Figure 7 shows that the proportion of asthma DALYs attributable to high BMI increased between 1990 and 2021. Globally, high-BMI-attributable asthma DALYs increased from **10.6% in 1990** to **14.9% in 2021**.

The attributable fraction was higher in females than males globally: **16.4% in females** compared with **13.3% in males**. Regional variation was also substantial, with the 2021 attributable fraction ranging from **10.8% to 26.5%** across regions.

The largest increase occurred in North Africa and the Middle East, where the high-BMI-attributable proportion increased from **17.3% in 1990** to **26.5% in 2021**. This suggests a growing contribution of metabolic risk to asthma-related disability burden, particularly in regions experiencing nutritional and lifestyle transitions.

These findings support the interpretation that asthma burden prevention should not focus exclusively on respiratory exposures. Weight control and metabolic risk reduction may also contribute to reducing asthma-related DALYs.

# Integrated Results Summary

This output sample shows that global age-standardized asthma burden declined between 1990 and 2021, but the decline was not uniform across indicators, regions, countries, age groups, sex groups, or SDI strata.

The key results are:

1. Global ASIR, ASPR, ASMR, and ASDR all declined between 1990 and 2021.

2. High-income North America had the highest incidence and prevalence-related burden, while Oceania had the highest mortality and DALY-related burden.

3. Several countries showed increasing AAPC values despite the global decline, including Oman, Poland, Barbados, Saudi Arabia, and Bermuda for ASIR; the United States for ASPR; Zimbabwe for ASMR and ASDR; and Lesotho for ASDR.

4. DALY burden differed substantially by age and sex, with high absolute burden in children and high rate-based burden among older adults.

5. SDI showed opposite associations with occurrence and outcome burden: higher SDI was associated with higher measured prevalence but lower mortality and DALY burden.

6. High BMI became a more prominent contributor to asthma-related DALYs, increasing globally from 10.6% to 14.9% between 1990 and 2021.

7. BAPC projections suggest persistent future asthma incidence through 2050, particularly across adult age groups.

Overall, the findings indicate that asthma burden has improved globally in standardized terms, but important inequalities persist. Regions with high prevalence require sustained chronic disease management, while regions with high mortality and DALY burden require improved diagnosis, access to treatment, acute care, and long-term control. The rising contribution of high BMI also suggests that metabolic and lifestyle interventions may become increasingly relevant to asthma burden reduction.
